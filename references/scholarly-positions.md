# Scholarly Positions: Calibrating the Verdict

There is no single Islamic ruling on cryptocurrency. The field holds three distinct camps. A good report doesn't pick a camp and declare — it locates the token within each camp's likely judgment, so the user sees the real range and can take an informed question to their own scholar. Read this before writing the "Where this lands" section.

---

## The three camps

### 1. Permissive camp ("default permissibility")
**Core reasoning:** Islamic law's baseline is *ibāḥah* — permissible unless specifically prohibited. Crypto isn't named in scripture, so absent a clear disqualifier it's allowed as a tradeable commodity (*māl mutaqawwim* / *'urūḍ*). Spot-holding involves no riba; decentralization avoids a central interest-charging authority.
**Where it lands on a token:** likely permits established, transparent, genuinely-useful tokens for spot holding. Will still object to a token that clearly fails maysir or has a haram underlying business — the default flips once a real disqualifier is present.
**Associated with:** Malaysia's Shariah Advisory Council (classified majors as commodities); scholars such as Mufti Faraz Adam and Ziyaad Mahomed have articulated conditional-permissibility reasoning in this direction; several South African scholars accepted major crypto as *māl*.

### 2. Conditional camp ("permissible only if…")
**Core reasoning:** crypto *can* be compliant, but only when specific conditions hold — genuine utility, transparency, no riba mechanics, acceptable speculation profile, and (for some) regulatory recognition. The token must earn permissibility, not inherit it by default.
**Where it lands on a token:** screens hard. Passes clean, useful, transparent, riba-free tokens; rejects opaque, hype-driven, or yield-bearing ones. This is the most analytically useful camp to reason in, because its conditions map directly onto the five screens.
**Associated with:** much of contemporary Islamic-finance scholarship and advisory practice (e.g. AAOIFI-aligned analysis, Shariah advisory firms). Mufti Taqi Usmani is broadly cautious/skeptical of speculative crypto but has left the door open for genuinely Shariah-compliant designs.

### 3. Prohibitive camp ("broadly impermissible")
**Core reasoning:** crypto's value rests on speculation and market sentiment (maysir/gharar), lacks intrinsic value or backing, is unregulated, and is readily used in illicit activity. Some hold it fails even the *māl*/money criteria.
**Where it lands on a token:** rejects most or all speculative tokens; may accept asset-backed designs (e.g. gold-backed) that address the intrinsic-value and speculation objections.
**Associated with:** the Grand Mufti of Egypt's office and Turkey's Diyanet (declared Bitcoin impermissible citing uncertainty and misuse); Indonesia's National Ulema Council (MUI) fatwa against crypto as currency citing *gharar* and wagering; scholars such as Shaykh Haitham al-Haddad on intrinsic-value grounds.

## How to phrase a calibrated verdict

Map the token's screen results onto the camps, then state the direction of the weight honestly. Templates by typical outcome:

**Token fails maysir + gharar (typical speculative micro-cap / memecoin):**
"Under the mainstream/conditional screening approach this would most likely be assessed as non-compliant — it fails the two screens scholars weight most heavily (maysir, gharar). The prohibitive camp reaches the same conclusion more firmly. Even the permissive camp, while not necessarily calling it *categorically* forbidden if some utility exists, would treat it as deeply questionable and not something to hold for speculative gain. Almost no recognized framework would call it cleanly halal in its current state."

**Token passes most screens (established, transparent, useful, riba-free):**
"This sits in the range the permissive and conditional camps would generally accept for spot holding, provided you avoid riba-bearing uses (lending, leverage, fixed yield). The prohibitive camp would still object on intrinsic-value/speculation grounds. The weight of mainstream screening leans permissible-for-holding, with the main caveat being how you use it."

**Token's risk is riba (stablecoin / yield product):**
"The decisive issue is riba, not maysir. If the backing/yield derives from interest-bearing instruments, the conditional and prohibitive camps would both object; a transparently non-interest-bearing design would fare much better. Verify the reserve/yield mechanics before concluding."

**Token's underlying business is haram:**
"This fails the underlying-activity screen outright — no camp permits a token whose underlying project is itself a prohibited activity, regardless of how clean the token's technical structure is."

Always include:
- **What would change the verdict** (the most useful sentence in the report) — e.g. published audits, a public team, genuine sustained utility, removal of yield mechanics, allocated/audited backing.
- **A pointer to a qualified scholar or Shariah advisory service** for a binding ruling, ideally with the tokenomics and disclosures in hand.

## Integrity guardrails

- **Don't invent fatwas.** If you're not certain a named scholar or body issued a specific ruling, describe the camp's *reasoning* generically rather than attributing a verdict. It's better to say "scholars in the prohibitive camp argue X" than to fabricate a citation.
- **Don't manufacture consensus.** The honest headline is that scholars disagree. Present the disagreement; don't flatten it.
- **Separate the token's theme from its mechanism.** An innocent theme doesn't rescue a speculative mechanism, and a scary theme doesn't condemn a sound structure. Judge the value mechanism and the underlying activity.
- **Respect the stakes.** This is a matter of conscience. Be rigorous, humble about the limits of your authority, and clear that the final word belongs to a qualified scholar.
