# Token Categories: Which Screens Bite, and the Red Flags for Each

The five screens apply universally, but they don't apply *evenly*. A stablecoin's risk is riba; a memecoin's risk is maysir. Categorize the token first, then read the matching section to know where to dig hardest and what red flags to hunt for. Many tokens are hybrids (a P2E memecoin, a yield-bearing stablecoin) — apply every relevant section.

---

## Memecoins / hype tokens

**Examples of the type:** dog/frog/celebrity coins, pump.fun bonding-curve launches, anything whose value narrative is "community and virality."

**Dominant screens:** Maysir (primary), Gharar (secondary), Utility (usually fails — little or none).

**Why they struggle:** Value is driven by social-media sentiment, FOMO, and momentum rather than utility or cash flow — which the dominant scholarly view treats as sharing the structure of gambling. Add anonymous teams and no audits and the gharar screen fails too. The mainstream assessment of this category is "highly problematic / avoid."

**Red flags:** bonding-curve / launchpad origin; anonymous team; no audit; concentrated insider holdings; extreme volatility on thin liquidity; value story is purely "community"; documented clone/scam tokens sharing the name.

**Nuance:** a minority view declines to call them *categorically* haram if any genuine utility exists, but even sympathetic scholars typically rate them as not barakah-linked and advise avoidance. Don't upgrade a memecoin to "fine" just because it has a thin veneer of utility.

## P2E / GameFi tokens

**Examples of the type:** play-to-earn game currencies, metaverse-land tokens, tokens you must hold or stake to play.

**Dominant screens:** Maysir (primary), Gharar (secondary), Utility (mixed), and sometimes the gambling-mechanics form of the underlying-activity screen.

**Why they're contested:** P2E is genuinely debated. Where earnings depend on buying in and hoping later demand lets you cash out higher — a payout-pool fed by new entrants — scholars see maysir-like structure. A mandatory hold-to-play requirement fuses "playing" with "speculating," so the token's value tracks sentiment from day one. The game being innocent (not a casino) helps the underlying-activity screen, but does **not** by itself clear maysir if the token economy is speculation-driven.

**Red flags:** rewards sustainability depends entirely on continued player inflow; mandatory token holding to participate; in-game mechanics that are themselves wagers (loot-box/lottery-style paid randomness with monetary value); anonymous team; no audit; treasury governance undisclosed.

**What helps:** a genuinely playable product with real, durable in-game utility and value sinks that don't rely on greater-fool dynamics; transparent team and treasury.

## Payment / "currency" coins

**Examples of the type:** coins positioned primarily as a medium of exchange or store of value with a long track record.

**Dominant screens:** Māl (the live debate), Maysir (if the user is purely speculating), Riba (only if used in lending/leverage).

**Why they're the most-accepted category:** the longest track records and deepest liquidity, and several advisory bodies have classified the majors as tradeable commodities (passing māl). The permissive and conditional camps often accept spot-holding; the prohibitive camp still objects on intrinsic-value and speculation grounds.

**Red flags:** the user intends leverage/margin (riba) or pure momentum day-trading (maysir) rather than holding/using; the specific coin is a low-quality imitation rather than an established asset.

---

## DeFi / yield / staking tokens

**Dominant screens:** Riba (primary), then Maysir and Gharar depending on the protocol.

**Why riba dominates:** lending protocols, fixed/guaranteed "savings" yields, and liquidity-provision returns can constitute interest. The critical distinction is whether a return is a *fixed charge for the use of money* (riba) or a *profit-share from genuine risk-bearing activity / payment for real work* (potentially permissible, closer to *muḍārabah*/*mushārakah* logic).

**Red flags:** advertised APY that is fixed and guaranteed regardless of underlying performance; "lending" wrappers; leverage; returns disconnected from any real economic activity. Complex, unaudited protocols also raise gharar.

**What helps:** returns explicitly tied to profit-and-loss sharing or genuine service provision, with transparent, audited mechanics.

---

## Stablecoins

**Dominant screens:** Riba (primary), Gharar (reserve transparency).

**Why riba dominates:** a fiat-pegged stablecoin is often backed by interest-bearing reserves (T-bills, interest-bearing deposits). Holders may be indirectly benefiting from riba even without a stated yield, and any yield-bearing stablecoin makes this explicit. Reserve opacity adds gharar (you can't verify what backs it).

**Red flags:** undisclosed or unaudited reserves; reserves known to be interest-bearing instruments; a yield-bearing design. **What helps:** transparent, audited, non-interest-bearing backing (some are designed specifically to avoid riba).

---

## Asset-backed / RWA (real-world-asset) tokens

**Examples of the type:** gold-backed tokens, tokenized commodities, tokenized real assets.

**Dominant screens:** Underlying asset's permissibility, Riba (in the redemption/backing mechanics), Gharar (custody and redemption clarity).

**Why this is often the strongest category:** backing by a tangible, halal asset (e.g. allocated gold) addresses the intrinsic-value objection and gives genuine *māl*. These are frequently cited as the most cleanly compliant crypto designs. But the structure must be sound: real, audited, allocated backing; clear redemption rights; no interest in the mechanics.

**Red flags:** the "backing" is unallocated, unaudited, or fractional; redemption is unclear; for gold/silver specifically, the exchange must respect the rules on currency-like commodities (spot settlement, no riba al-faḍl). **What helps:** allocated, audited, fully-backed reserves with transparent custody and redemption.

---

## Governance / utility tokens (infrastructure, protocols)

**Dominant screens:** Utility (genuine?), Maysir (if traded purely speculatively), Underlying-activity (what does the protocol actually do?).

**Why they vary widely:** a governance token for a genuinely useful, halal protocol can score well; one whose only real use is speculative trading falls back on the maysir screen. Always check what the protocol *does* — a governance token for a gambling or interest-lending protocol inherits that protocol's impermissibility.

**Red flags:** "utility" that is purely circular (the token's only use is to be traded/staked for more tokens); the underlying protocol facilitates a prohibited activity.

---

## NFT-linked tokens

**Dominant screens:** Underlying-activity (what does the NFT represent?), Gharar, Maysir.

**Notes:** an NFT representing a halal good/right with clear terms is more defensible; one tied to gambling mechanics, prohibited imagery, or pure speculative flipping is not. Mint mechanics that are lottery-like (paid randomized reveals with monetary stakes) raise maysir directly.

---

## Cross-cutting red flags (any category)

- Anonymous / unverifiable team
- No security audit, or unaudited contracts with mint/freeze authority retained
- Opaque treasury or governance
- Documented scam clones, fake websites, or impersonation
- Tokenomics with heavy insider concentration or hidden unlocks
- Value sustained only by continuous new-buyer inflow (greater-fool structure)
- The user's intended use adds leverage, margin, or interest-bearing yield
- The underlying business is itself a prohibited activity

Any single cross-cutting flag should appear in the report; a cluster of them strongly pushes the gharar and maysir screens toward failure.
